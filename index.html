INFER: INtermediate representations for FuturE pRediction
Shashank Srikanth, Junaid Ahmed Ansari, Karnik Ram, Sarthak Sharma, J Krishna Murthy, K Madhava Krishna
IROS 2019
arxiv / project page / code / video /

In this work, we proposed intermediate representations and an autoregressive model to accurately predict future trajectories of traffic participants (vehicles). We demonstrate that using semantics provides a significant boost and allows the model generalize to completely different datasets, collected across several cities, and also across countries where people drive on opposite sides of the road (left-handed vs right-handed driving).

Don't cross that stop line: Characterizing Traffic Violations in Metropolitan Cities
Shashank Srikanth, Aanshul Sadaria, Himanshu Bhatia, Kanay Gupta, Pratik Jain, Ponnurangam Kumaraguru,
Under review at CODS COMAD, 2020
arxiv

In this work, we describe a method of collecting e-challan (Electronic traffic-violation receipt) data from the e-challan portal of Ahmedabad traffic police and analyze over 3 million e-challans to charecterize spatial and temporal patterns. We also characterize user behaviour with respect to repeat offenses and fine payment and propose a set of 5 features to model recidivism in traffic violations. We train and evaluate multiple classifiers on these proposed features and achieve 95% accuracy on the test set.

Is change the only constant? Profile change perspective on #LokSabhaElections2019
Neha Kumari, Shashank Srikanth, Sonali Singhal, Shwetanshu Singh, Arun Balaji Buduru, Ponnurangam Kumaraguru,
Under review at CODS COMAD, 2020
arxiv

In this work, we consider #LokSabhaElections2019 as a movement and perform a large-scale study of the profile of users who actively made changes to profile attributes centered around #LokSabhaElections2019. We collect the profile metadata for 49.4 M users on Twitter for a period of 2 months from April 5, 2019 to June 5, 2019 amid #LokSabhaElections2019, and investigate the data to differentiate between organic and inorganic ways to show political inclination from the prism of profile changes. We also study the use of election campaign keywords in the user profile and characterize it as a form of behaviour contagion.

Detecting and charecterizing distracted driving content on Snapchat
Shashank Srikanth, Dheeraj Reddy Pailla, Shwetanshu Singh, Hemank Lamba, Ponnurangam Kumaraguru

In this work, we proposed a deep learning model to detect distracted driving snaps (videos) on Snapchat, a popular social media network. We further collect a dataset of over 6 million snaps (videos) from 173 cities around the world using the SnapMaps API. The labels provided by the above trained classifier were used to infer spatial and temporal patterns of distracted driving content. We also analyze how distracted driving content posting behavior changes due to varied demographics using factors put forward by frameworks like Edgework.
